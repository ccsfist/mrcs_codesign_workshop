# 3b. Understanding Community Climate Impacts in Madagascar

## 5. Recap of climate science analysis

Madagascar's climate risks are highly heterogeneous, with different regions exposed to distinct hazard profiles shaped by geography, atmospheric circulation, and oceanic influences. According to analyses from the Integrated Food Security Phase Classification (IPC)[^1], climate shocks remain among the most important drivers of livelihood disruptions across the country, affecting agricultural production, livestock systems, natural resources, and rural economies. Furthermore, IPC emphasizes that acute food insecurity in the Grand Sud and Grand Sud-Est “*is driven by a combination of recurring climate shocks (including droughts, floods, and cyclones)*.” Because much of the population relies on rain-fed subsistence agriculture, and climate-dependent pastoralism and fisheries, the island's hyper-regionalized climate directly governs these sector outputs.  

The Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research[^2] identified three broad climate-risk zones that are particularly relevant for understanding vulnerability and adaptation pathways in Madagascar. The semi-arid Grand Sud is particularly exposed to recurrent droughts and prolonged dry spells, while the Grand Sud-Est faces frequent cyclones, flooding, and coastal hazards associated with the Indian Ocean. Northern and northwestern regions are strongly influenced by monsoonal rainfall systems, where delayed rainfall onset, uneven precipitation, and cyclone-related flooding can disrupt agricultural production. Despite these regional differences, a common challenge across Madagascar is increasing climate variability, which affects rainfall timing, water availability, agricultural productivity, livestock systems, fisheries, and natural resource-dependent livelihoods. 

Several studies, including Climate change linked to drought in Southern Madagascar[^3], suggest that climate change may increase drought severity in the southern region through rising temperatures, increased evapotranspiration, and longer dry periods. While projected changes in average rainfall remain uncertain, warmer conditions are expected to intensify the impacts of rainfall deficits when droughts occur. Recent drought episodes in the Grand Sud illustrate how repeated dry years can compound livelihood losses and reduce recovery capacity.

From an adaptation planning perspective, the most important challenge may not be changes in average climate conditions, but rather the possibility that droughts, cyclones, and other climate shocks become more frequent, more intense, or occur in closer succession. Understanding how communities respond to climate variability today provides an important foundation for evaluating adaptation options under future climate conditions.

## 6. Farmer Input / Community Data

The community climate survey revealed important differences between Manakara, Toliara I, and Toliara II, reflecting distinct livelihood systems, climate hazards, and vulnerability profiles. While drought emerged as the most frequently reported hazard across all communities, the frequency of bad years over the past decade and their livelihood impacts varied considerably according to local ecological and socio-economic conditions.

### ***Agricultural Communities – Manakara***

Manakara communities are predominantly dependent on rice, maize, cassava, and yam production. Over the past ten years, communities reported approximately 6 bad years out of 10 (6/10) associated with droughts, excessive rainfall, and cyclones. The most frequently cited bad years were 2019, 2020, 2021, 2022, and 2023, with 2022 identified as the most severe year due to the combined effects of drought and Cyclones Batsirai and Emnati. Droughts occurring between January and mid-February affected yam planting and rice establishment, while excessive rainfall in March and May disrupted maize production and rice flowering. Cyclones caused widespread crop destruction, increased crop diseases, and reduced household food security. Consequently, agriculture in Manakara exhibits a high sensitivity to both rainfall deficits and excess rainfall during critical crop growth stages. The overall frequency of reported agricultural bad years can therefore be estimated at approximately 60% of the last decade (6/10 years).

### ***Pastoral and Agro-Pastoral Communities – Toliara I***

Toliara I communities depend largely on livestock production, agro-pastoralism, and maize cultivation. Drought was overwhelmingly identified as the dominant hazard, with communities reporting approximately 7 bad years out of 10 (7/10) due to recurring rainfall deficits. The most frequently cited bad years included 2016, 2018, 2019, 2020, 2021, and 2022, reflecting the prolonged drought conditions that affected southern Madagascar. Drought resulted in reduced pasture availability, declining water resources, livestock migration, reduced herd productivity, and lower maize yields. Although excess rainfall and cyclones such as Cyclone Haruna (2013) were mentioned, they occurred less frequently than droughts. Compared to the other study sites, Toliara I exhibited the highest frequency of climate-related bad years because of its strong dependence on climate-sensitive rangelands. The overall frequency of reported bad years is estimated at approximately 70% of the last decade (7/10 years).

### ***Coastal Fishing Communities – Toliara II***

Toliara II communities are characterized by a strong dependence on fisheries, coastal resources, salt production, and small-scale trade. Communities reported approximately 5 bad years out of 10 (5/10), with cyclones and coastal storms representing the most significant hazards. The most frequently cited bad years were 2013 (Cyclone Haruna), 2022 (Cyclones Batsirai and Emnati), and 2023 (Cyclone Freddy). Unlike Toliara I, drought impacts were largely indirect, affecting water availability and coastal ecosystems rather than directly reducing fish catches. Cyclones caused extensive damage to fishing boats, fishing gear, landing sites, coastal infrastructure, and mangrove ecosystems that support fisheries productivity. Excess rainfall during January and February occasionally disrupted fishing activities and local markets. Overall, climate-related bad years in Toliara II were less frequent than in Toliara I but generally produced more severe episodic impacts when major cyclones occurred. The estimated frequency of bad years is approximately 50% of the last decade (5/10 years).

### **Comparative Frequency and Livelihood Impacts**

A comparison across the three livelihood systems shows that Toliara I recorded the highest frequency of bad years (7/10 years) due to recurrent drought and rangeland degradation. Manakara recorded approximately 6/10 bad years, reflecting its exposure to multiple hazards including droughts, floods, and cyclones that affect crop production. Toliara II recorded approximately 5/10 bad years, with major losses concentrated around severe cyclone years. While drought was the most recurrent hazard across all communities, cyclones generated the greatest livelihood losses when they occurred. Agriculture was most vulnerable in Manakara, pastoralism in Toliara I, and fisheries and coastal livelihoods in Toliara II. These findings highlight the need for livelihood-specific anticipatory action and adaptation strategies tailored to each community’s dominant hazard profile and seasonal risk calendar.

## 7. Madagascar National Adaptation Plan and MRCS operational approaches

The climate risks identified in the literature and echoed by communities across southern Madagascar have increasingly shaped national adaptation priorities. Recurrent droughts, cyclones, rainfall variability, land degradation, and pressure on climate-sensitive livelihoods have highlighted the need for adaptation approaches that strengthen both resilience and risk management. In response, Madagascar’s National Adaptation Plan (NAP)[^4] identifies 3 priority sector actions:  agriculture and food security, forestry and biodiversity, and water and health. The NAP promotes a combination of climate-smart agriculture, sustainable natural resource management, ecosystem-based adaptation, climate information services, and strengthened disaster risk governance. 

Many of these priorities are reflected in the operational approaches implemented by the Malagasy Red Cross Society (MRCS)[^5]. Through partnerships with government agencies, IFRC, and development actors, MRCS supports community-based interventions that combine disaster preparedness, anticipatory action, livelihood resilience, and ecosystem restoration. Current activities include early warning dissemination, simulation exercises, forecast-based planning, livelihood diversification initiatives, climate-smart agriculture, fisheries support, and nature-based solutions such as mangrove restoration and coastal reforestation.

Together, the NAP and MRCS programming reflect a growing recognition that adaptation must address both long-term climate change and recurrent climate variability. However, many adaptation measures require time, resources, and favorable conditions to generate benefits. As climate shocks become more frequent or occur in closer succession, there is increasing interest in understanding how these interventions perform under stress and whether additional risk-management approaches may be needed to protect livelihoods and support recovery
